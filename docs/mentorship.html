APA National Planning Conference DataJam

Challenge Question: How can sites that are administrative locations rather than service locations be identified in FacDB?

I partnered with BetaNYC at the APA National Planning Conference Datajam to ideate a technical methodology for improving location confidence intervals in NYC facilities database.

The Problem:
  • Misleading Locations: Locations may be inaccurate due to data sources (may refer to org mailing/billing address instead of service location)
  • Inconsistency: Community based orgs do not have consistent service locations & may provide services at administrative location (unable to make sweeping assumptions)

We were able to successfully geocode >6k sites and plot using Mapzen. From there, we presented a four-part methodology:

  1. Geocode admin addresses, cross-reference with facilities program sites
  2. Split by [category] (FACgroup? FACsubgrp?) & Eliminate subgroups that are more likely to be verified (i.e., parks, housing, schools, etc.)
  3. Manual classification for training set (validate admin service locations vs. service locations) — should be done by subject matter experts. (a. Remove/examine duplicates, b. Use zoning restrictions for location validation)
  4. Run through ML models for testing set CI
Datasets Used:
  • Administrative addresses from HHS Accelerator: Known administrative addresses for orgs with City contracts and provide social services
  • Operator’s websites and Access NYC API data: Program info provided by organizations and website urls

The methodology was taken internally by the NYC facilities team.

HeatSeek NYC

HeatSeek is a civic tech nonprofit dedicated to "empowering tenants, landlords, community organizations, and the justice system to tackle our city’s heating crisis". The team places low-cost heat sensors in local residential units to track temperature data and monitor via a webapp. In doing this, they hold landlords accountable, inform housing policy, and provide evidence to verify heating code abuse claims in housing court.

I was approached by HeatSeek to create a prototype data analytics dashboard comparing public 311 heating complaints with HeatSeek sensors. The team was interested in using Power BI for their analytics because of its ease of use in generating analytics for varying technical abilities. The resulting dashboard can be viewed on the left. There are four main functions:

  • NYC Trends: Shows rate of change for complaints by zip code
  • Violation Analysis: Shows addresses with most complaints & their locations
  • Violation Search: Search tool for monthly violation count per address or apartment unit
  • Apartment Search: Search tool tracking indoor vs. outdoor temperatures by address or apartment unit

For more on HeatSeek, see here.

NYC Parks: Data Mentorship

2016 National Day of Civic Hacking

My team sponsored the NYC Parks TreesCount! DataJam, prompting the city’s civic technology, data, and design communities to to make sense of New York’s latest and most spatially accurate urban forest dataset. The census mapped 530,000 trees, including the entirety of Manhattan, The Bronx, and Staten Island (80% of all NYC streets). Some goals and challenges revolved around quantifying how NYC’s forest has changed over time, understanding how the census can be correlated with local economic and environmental indicators, increasing civic stewardship/education, and improving its current and future health. I wrote a blog post recapping my team's participation and project submission here.

Wogrammer NY

Head of Operations

Wogrammer NY was the first local chapter to celebrate, support, and connect accomplished women in tech in the New York area. The organization (Endorsed by Sheryl Sandberg) was initially founded by two Facebook engineers looking to break stereotypes around women in technology. I served as the Head of Operations for Wogrammer NY. During this time, I organized and executed the launch event (Hosted by Facebook NY).

Duties Included:
  • Program curation: Keynote, speakers, lineup, etc.
  • Event catering
  • Advertising/Press

After the launch, I was responsible for contacting, interviewing, and publishing Wogrammer NY features on the dedicated Facebook fan page. I was also responsible for delegating Facebook ad credits to optimize user engagement. Eventually, chapters began to spawn around the country. I helped organize and curate their respective launches and features. The chapters have since been discontinued, but the project lives on through Twitter and Medium.

Civic Hacker Hours

Civic Hacker Hours was created from a want to further connect technical workers in the NYC area. I established a weekly event at Civic Hall where anyone could come and share projects, ask questions, and work collaboratively. By creating a civic focus, Hacker Hours attendees all came with similar intent. At the meetups, I provided hosting, catering, and technical mentorship. I also scheduled speakers to present projects, tutorials, and open-call hackathon contributions. For those interested in starting a career in civic tech, I held office hours for any questions and/or recommendations. The Civic Hacker Hours event was created from the Hacker Hours meetup community.
