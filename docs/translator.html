Microsoft Translator

Background

Recently, my team has been interested in the opportunity to use Microsoft's free language translation tools to help New Yorkers access government services by lowering language barriers. We decided to pilot Microsoft Translator, a tool that offers voice and text translation in over 60 languages and works without a data connection. The City expressed interest in dedicating these resources towards New York City's municipal identification services, IDNYC.

IDNYC is the largest municipal identification card program in the nation with over 700,000 approved card applications processed at 18 locations across the city. Because New York City is home to eight million residents who speak over 800 languages, it was clear IDNYC offered a unique opportunity to assist staff and applicants with language translation.

To address this, we piloted Microsoft Translator around IDNYC registration centers in the NYC area. Members of our team conducted a two-hour long training session at the beginning of the pilot period as well as in-person visits at each location three to four times per week. This proved useful in assisting with tool usage, handling technical difficulties, and getting personal feedback from IDNYC staff. My responsibilities for this pilot were as follows:

  • Programmed desktop microphones to accept keyboard input
  • Configured surfaces for IDNYC employees
  • Assisted building registration splash page and custom userscripts
  • Provided technical training for IDNYC staff (sole member training most employees)
  • Provided on-site support during technical difficulties for both locations
  • Set up feedback form and analyzed data
  • Researched and ordered microphone and headset equipment
  • Ordered and programed NFC stickers for misc. demos
  • Built and maintained positive relationships with city employees

Usage

During each use, an IDNYC staff member and applicant would sit across from each other at a desk, each with their own Surface device and desktop microphone. My team built a splashpage which prompted the applicant with an agreement form. The applicant selected their preferred language and localized terms of use. From there, we built custom userscripts to auto-populate API parameters to create an easier product flow for users. To speak, each user held down the button on the desktop mic and spoke clearly. At the end of the session, the user exited the conversation and the conversation cache was wiped.

Privacy concerns were [understandably] a priority for IDNYC employees. To combat this, Translator scrubs anything that sounds like a name or number (SSN, telephone number, etc) and saves sample sizes of non-consecutive words. Of course, logging can be omitted entirely using the API.

Overall, the pilot was positively received by staff and visitors. Spanish and French translations received especially positive reviews. In addition to Mandarin translation, the ease of the user experience each received mixed reviews that indicate they could be improved, particularly for occasions in which one or more users do not already expect translation technology to be involved.

Feedback and Reflection

We encouraged staff to fill out feedback forms with a series of self-reported metrics on how successful the encounter had been. One of the initial findings by TCE was that conversations did not align neatly into English-only and pure foreign language. A number of visitors spoke some English but were not fluent. For these users, Translator proved helpful for particular sentences, snippets, or even individual words. So while some users tapped into the full potential of Translator, others benefited from a hybrid approach that only used Translator intermittently. Hand gestures continued to be common during the use of Translator.

Overall, IDNYC was receptive to the pilot and staff seemed eager to further integrate. Translator received an 8.08 utility satisfaction rate, a 7.33 satisfaction rate for ease of use, and a 7.67 satisfaction rate for whether the tool assisted with conversation flow.

New York City, like many municipalities, currently uses Language Line Solutions for translation services – at a cost of $4/minute. Calculating estimated usage, that adds up to $60/day and$300/week in translation costs for each and every location. We found that the use of Translator would cost less than two weeks of the current system. Over the course of a year, a single IDNYC location could save $15,600, adding up to a total of $280,800 annually across the entire program.

Future Work

  • Onsite canvassing using Translator for healthcare registration
  • Establish & build relationships with city employees
